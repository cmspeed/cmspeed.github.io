ABOUT

I am a PhD Candidate in the Department of Geological Sciences at the University of Texas at Austin and a Graduate Research Assistant in the Quantitative Clastics Laboratory (QCL) at the Bureau of Economic Geology. I graduated with a degree in Geophysics from UT-Austin in 2017, where I completed an honors thesis on the seismic stratigraphy of the Gulf of Mexico continental shelf.

My current Ph.D. research projects leverage a variety of remote sensing datasets and methods to quantify linkages between the processes controlling the morphologic and topographic evolution of planetary landscapes and the expression of these processes in the sedimentary record. A majority of my work to date has focused on establishing these linkages within modern fluvial environments on Earth and ancient preserved fluvial deposits on Earth and Mars through the integration of lidar and structure-from-motion derived 3-D point cloud data, time-lapse satellite imagery, kinematic models, and field measurements. Using these methods, I aim to develop predictive models of both the evolution of planetary landscapes and the distribution of sediments and pore space within the subsurface for applications related to infrastructure planning, energy exploration / carbon sequestration, and interpretations of past planetary surface conditions.

My research interests have continued to evolve over the last few years, as I have started to dive deeper into the development of Python-based workflows for processing, analyzing, and visualizing geospatial datasets for my own use and that of the broader scientific community. Recently, I've started exploring image segmentation and classification approaches for the automated identification of landforms from high resolution satellite imagery with plans of further developing and applying these methods in my academic and professional career.
